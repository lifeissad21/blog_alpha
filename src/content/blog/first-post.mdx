In October, I gave a short talk in an intro seminar class to over 300 freshmen studying Computer Science at Georgia Tech titled "Transformative AI, risk, and you." The talk asked them to extrapolate AI progress and internalize that AI will very soon impact their careers and lives in ways (and perhaps extremely negative ways) they should start thinking about now.

It seemed well-received, and recently I was interviewed by Jason Armesto at the AJC about the talk and how college students are thinking about job loss from AI. [The article](https://www.ajc.com) came out well - it does a good job at capturing lots of different perspectives and getting across the varied vibes across campuses.

## The Current State of AI

There was one quotation in the article that I thought was particularly interesting: it highlighted how students are not only worried about AI replacing their jobs, but also about how to stay relevant in a rapidly changing landscape. This is a legitimate concern that I think more people should be discussing openly.

## What This Means for Your Career

The key takeaway from my talk and the subsequent interview is this: **start thinking about these issues now**. Don't wait until you're 5 years into your career to realize that the field has completely transformed.

Some concrete steps you can take:

- Stay curious about AI developments
- Build projects that showcase adaptability
- Develop skills that are harder to automate
- Network with people across different fields
- Consider the ethical implications of technology

## Looking Forward

As we move into this new era of AI capability, the students I spoke with seemed to understand the gravity of the situation. They're not panicking, but they're also not ignoring it. That's probably the healthiest response we can have.

The conversation about AI and unemployment is going to become increasingly important as we see more real-world applications and deployment. I'm glad the AJC article helped start this dialogue on a broader scale.
